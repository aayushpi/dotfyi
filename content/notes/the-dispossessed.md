A physicist from an anarchist moon travels to the lush, capitalist planet his people fled generations ago. Le Guin uses the double narrative — alternating between Shevek's life on Anarres and his journey on Urras — to show that no society cleanly resolves the tension between individual freedom and collective obligation.

What stuck with me most is the way scarcity is treated not just as a material condition but as a psychological one. The Anarresti have internalized a kind of moral scarcity — a suspicion of pleasure, of accumulation, of standing out — that ends up reproducing the oppression they escaped. Freedom requires more than different rules; it requires different wants.

The ending refuses easy resolution, which is the correct choice.
